# Deep research with citations — evidence, not vibes

When a decision needs real research, produce a cited synthesis — every claim traceable to a source.
This is the no-fabrication rule applied to research.

## Workflow
1. **Frame** — restate the goal in one line; is this for learning, a decision, or writing? If genuinely ambiguous, ask 1–2 narrowing questions; else proceed with sensible defaults.
2. **Decompose** — break the topic into **3–5 sub-questions**. Research targets the sub-questions, not the vague whole.
3. **Search wide** — per sub-question, run **2–3 keyword variations**, mix general + recent/news, aim for **15–30 unique sources** total. Priority ladder: academic / official / reputable news > blogs > forums.
4. **Read deep** — full-read **3–5 of the most promising sources**. Don't synthesize from snippets.
5. **Synthesize** — a report with: a header line `Sources: N | Confidence: High/Med/Low`, a 3–5 sentence executive summary, numbered themes each with **inline `[source](url)` citations**, key takeaways, and the source list.

## Quality rules (these are the point)
- Every claim has a source.
- A claim found in only ONE source is flagged **unverified** until corroborated.
- Prefer last-12-months recency; note when evidence is stale.
- Acknowledge gaps — "insufficient data found" beats inventing an answer.
- **Separate fact from inference** — label estimates, projections, and opinions as such. Never present a projection as a measured fact.

## If you can spawn subagents (BRAIN=claude)
Fan out ~3 research subagents, each owning a couple sub-questions; the main session synthesizes and de-dupes. (Prefer a dedicated deep-research skill/tool end-to-end when one is available.)
